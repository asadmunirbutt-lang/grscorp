## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Spectrum Arch, Inc. is a newly incorporated nonprofit organization dedicated to developing and operating residential living facilities and support services for adults with autism spectrum disorder in the Capital Region of New York State (Saratoga, Albany, and Rensselaer Counties).

**Mission:** To promote the independence, dignity, well-being, and community integration of adults with autism spectrum disorder through the provision of safe, affirming residential housing and comprehensive support services.

**Model:** Individual Residential Alternative (IRA) — a state-certified, Medicaid-funded model serving 5–8 residents per home with 24-hour supportive oversight and daytime intensive support.

**Target Population:** Adults aged 21–55 with autism spectrum disorder and medium-to-high support needs, primarily from families in the Capital Region seeking stable, long-term residential options.

**Revenue Model:** 100% government-funded through New York State Medicaid and OPWDD reimbursement. No resident or family fees. Expected reimbursement: $180,000–$220,000 per resident annually.

**Year 1 Goals:**
- Secure 501(c)(3) tax-exempt status from IRS
- Complete OPWDD new provider orientation and begin CRO certification process
- Establish board governance and operational policies
- Secure initial grants (Google Ad Grants, Microsoft grants, foundation grants)
- Identify and secure property lease in Saratoga/Capital Region
- Build operational foundation for Year 2 launch

**Year 2 Goals:**
- Achieve OPWDD provider certification
- Hire core staff (Program Director, DSPs, Admin)
- Admit first 3–4 residents
- Stabilize operations and gather outcome data

**Year 3 Goals:**
- Scale to full capacity (5–8 residents)
- Demonstrate success metrics to funders and OPWDD
- Explore replication (second home) if demand warrants

## SECTION 2: THE IRA MODEL — WHAT WE OPERATE

### What is an IRA?
An Individual Residential Alternative (IRA) is a state-certified residential service model under New York's Office for People With Developmental Disabilities (OPWDD). Key characteristics:

- **Size:** 2–8 residents in a single home or small multi-unit building
- **Staffing:** 24-hour staff presence (evening/night staff available on-call or on-site)
- **Residents:** Adults with developmental disabilities (autism, intellectual disabilities, etc.)
- **Services:** Residential habilitation, community integration support, daily living skills support
- **Funding:** 100% through Medicaid (New York State) — no private fees
- **Certification:** Required by OPWDD; includes annual inspections, staff training requirements, licensing

### IRA vs. Other Models

| Factor | IRA | Supported Living | Integrated Supportive Housing (ISH) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Residents per Home** | 2–8 | 1–2 (independent apts) | Varies (partnership model) |
| **Staff Presence** | 24/7 or on-call | Daytime/as-needed visits | Daytime/evening visits |
| **Ownership** | Nonprofit or government | Resident/private | Housing developer; org provides services |
| **Certification** | OPWDD required | Not required | Not required |
| **Medicaid Rate** | High (~$180–220K/person/yr) | Moderate (~$100–150K/person/yr) | Variable |
| **Startup Complexity** | Medium | Low | High (partnership negotiations) |
| **Best For** | Medium-high support needs | Higher independence | Scaling multiple units |

**Why Spectrum Arch chose IRA:**
1. Highest Medicaid reimbursement (covers all operational costs)
2. Most direct funding stream (fewer intermediaries)
3. Clearest regulatory pathway (OPWDD certification process well-defined)
4. Largest population of medium-high need adults with autism
5. Best outcomes for individuals with daily support needs

## SECTION 3: TARGET POPULATION & MARKET NEED

### Who We Serve

**Primary Population:**
- **Age:** 21–55 years old
- **Diagnosis:** Autism Spectrum Disorder (confirmed diagnosis per DSM-5)
- **Support Level:** Medium to high — require daily support but not intensive medical care
- **Geography:** Saratoga, Albany, Rensselaer Counties (Capital Region, New York)

**Specific Support Needs:**
- Require assistance with medication management and health monitoring
- Need support for meal planning, grocery shopping, meal preparation
- Require daily prompting/assistance with personal hygiene and grooming
- Need support for money management and budgeting
- Benefit from community integration and social support
- May have part-time employment with job coaching support
- Require behavioral/emotional support and structure
- Need assistance with medical and dental appointments

### Market Need & Demand

**The Crisis:** Thousands of young adults with autism in New York State reach age 21 and "fall off a cliff" — school services end, but no residential housing or day programs exist. Most remain with aging parents (often now in their 70s–80s) with no plan for the future.

**Capital Region Demand:**
- Approximately **2,000+ adults with autism** in Saratoga, Albany, Rensselaer Counties
- Approximately **500–700** are in the 21–55 age range with medium-high support needs
- Currently, **fewer than 50 residential slots** exist in the Capital Region
- **Waiting list for OPWDD residential services:** 18–36 months in most regions
- **Family desperation:** Parents actively seeking safe, affordable, quality housing options

**Competitive Landscape:**
- Few nonprofit providers specifically focused on autism (most are generic IDD providers)
- Existing providers often have years-long waiting lists
- Significant unmet need and family advocacy for more options

### Why This Population?

1. **Largest unmet need:** Young adults with autism have fewest service options
2. **Highest market demand:** Families most actively seeking housing solutions
3. **Optimal support level:** Medium-high needs generate highest Medicaid reimbursement
4. **Clear outcomes:** Autism diagnosis makes impact measurement straightforward
5. **OPWDD priority:** NY State actively funds autism service expansion

## SECTION 4: OPERATIONAL PLAN

### Residential Model — A Typical 6-Resident IRA

**Physical Space:**
- One single-family home or duplex with 6 private or semi-private bedrooms
- Common areas: kitchen, dining, living room, activity space
- Laundry facilities, accessible bathrooms
- Safe, accessible, ADA-compliant
- Located in community (not institutional setting)
- Lease-based (no property ownership required initially)

**Daily Operations:**
- Residents wake independently or with morning reminders/support
- Breakfast prepared by staff with resident participation
- Morning hygiene & grooming support
- Some residents attend day programs (day habilitation, employment)
- Staff manage medications, appointments, community outings
- Evening meal prep & eating together (community building)
- Evening activities (TV, games, community events, family visits)
- Bedtime routines & overnight oversight
- Weekend community integration (shopping, recreation, family visits, faith activities)

### Staffing Model (6-Resident Home)

| Position | FTE | Annual Salary | Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Program Director / Manager | 1.0 | $55,000 | Oversight, regulatory compliance, hiring, budget |
| Lead DSP (Senior/Shift Lead) | 1.0 | $42,000 | Evening shift supervision, training, mentoring |
| DSP (Evening/Community Support) | 1.5 | $42,000 | Direct care, community outings, meal prep, supervision |
| DSP (Day/Daytime Support) | 1.5 | $42,000 | Day habilitation coordination, employment support, appointments |
| Night Staff (Overnight Awake) | 0.5 | $40,000 | Overnight supervision, emergencies, health monitoring |
| Administrative / Bookkeeper | 0.5 | $38,000 | Scheduling, billing, record-keeping, compliance |
| **TOTAL** | **6.0 FTE** | **~$259,000** | **Fully covers resident care** |

**Key Points:**
- **All salaries paid from Medicaid reimbursement** — no startup capital needed
- **Training covered:** All DSPs receive OPWDD-mandated Frontline Staff Training (paid by org)
- **Program Director qualifications:** Bachelor's degree, social work/psychology/education background preferred
- **DSP qualifications:** High school diploma/GED minimum; OPWDD training on-the-job
- **Hiring timeline:** 3 months before first residents (allows training, team-building)

### Services Provided

**Residential Habilitation (Core Service — Medicaid-funded):**
- Safe, stable housing with trained staff
- Daily living skills instruction (cooking, cleaning, hygiene, budgeting)
- Medication management & health oversight
- Behavioral support & crisis intervention training
- Community integration support

**Day Habilitation (Optional — if residents need it):**
- Daytime activities, job coaching, volunteering
- Can be provided in-house or coordinated with external providers
- Some residents may attend external day programs (Medicaid funds those separately)

**Community Habilitation:**
- Support for employment (job coach, employer liaison)
- Community participation (recreation, shopping, faith, social activities)
- Transition support (independent living skills)

**Coordination Services:**
- Connection with medical providers, mental health, therapy
- Family communication & support
- OPWDD liaison & reporting

### Quality Standards & Outcomes

Spectrum Arch will track and report on:
- **Person-centered outcomes:** Resident goals met, independence gains, community participation
- **Safety metrics:** Incident rates, medication accuracy, emergency response time
- **Compliance metrics:** Regulatory standards met, inspections passed
- **Satisfaction:** Resident & family satisfaction surveys, feedback incorporation
- **Staff metrics:** Retention rate, training completion, staff satisfaction

## SECTION 8: RISK MITIGATION

### Risk 1: Delay in OPWDD Certification
**Risk:** OPWDD processes CRO application slowly; property issues delay inspection
**Mitigation:** 
- Apply early (Q3 2026) to account for delays
- Build 3–4 month buffer into timeline
- Maintain relationship with DDRO throughout process
- Have backup property options identified

### Risk 2: Property Scarcity/High Costs
**Risk:** Suitable accessible properties hard to find or lease rates exceed projections
**Mitigation:**
- Begin search 6 months before needed (early Q4 2026)
- Budget flexibility ($5,000–$8,000/month for lease)
- Consider partnering with housing developers (ISH model later)
- Explore HUD Section 811 supportive housing program

### Risk 3: Staff Recruitment & Retention
**Risk:** Difficulty hiring experienced DSPs; turnover reduces continuity
**Mitigation:**
- Hire Program Director early to lead recruitment
- Offer competitive salaries ($40–45K for DSP)
- Build strong workplace culture & professional development
- Partner with community colleges for DSP pipeline training

### Risk 4: Medicaid Reimbursement Cuts
**Risk:** State reduces OPWDD funding; rate decreases mid-year
**Mitigation:**
- Build 5–10% operational reserve by Year 3
- Diversify revenue (grants, donations)
- Track policy changes; lobby through advocacy groups
- Model scenarios with lower reimbursement rates

### Risk 5: Resident Crises or Safety Issues
**Risk:** Residents experience mental health crises, injuries, or behavioral incidents
**Mitigation:**
- Comprehensive staff training (de-escalation, first aid, CPR)
- 24/7 access to crisis support (partnering with OPWDD crisis team)
- Clear policies for incident reporting & response
- Regular supervision & consultation for staff

### Risk 6: Regulatory Non-Compliance
**Risk:** OPWDD inspection finds violations; certification at risk
**Mitigation:**
- Build compliance into operations from day 1
- Regular internal audits
- Maintain detailed documentation & records
- Establish relationships with regulatory bodies
- Annual compliance training for staff
